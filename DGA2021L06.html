Lecture 06

Vector-fields

Let $M$ be a $k$-dimensional manifold. We denote the set of vector-fields on $M$ by $\mathfrak{X}(M)$ or by $\Gamma(M,TM)$ (i.e. sections of $TM$ defined on $M$).

As we noticed, tangent vectors on $\mathbb{R}^k$ correspond to directional derivatives, therefore they can be written as $$A=A^1(p)\partial_1+A^2(p)\partial_2+\ldots+A^k(p)\partial_k$$ where the components $A^j:\mathbb{R}^k\to\mathbb{R}$ are smooth functions and $\partial_j$ is the partial derivative with respect to the $j$-th coordinate. The set $$\{\partial_1,\ldots, \partial_k\}$$ gives, at every point, a basis for $T_p\mathbb{R}^k$; it is called a global frame.

If $(U,\phi)$ is a chart for $M$, then $(TM\vert_U,(\phi,\phi_*))$ is a chart for $TM$; the functions $\phi^*x^j=x^j\circ\phi$ give coordinates on $U$ and the vector-fields $X_j\in \Gamma(U,TM)$, such that $\phi_*X_j(x^h)=\delta_j^h$ give at each point of $U$ a basis of $T_pM$.

The vector-fields $X_1,\ldots, X_k$, sections of $TM$ on $U$, are a local frame.

Local computations

Once we have a local frame, we can perform local computations. If we are dealing with the "right" quantities, results will be independent of the local frame we chose, so we optimistically omit it.

Therefore, the vector-field $A=A^j\partial_j$ (Einstein summation: repeated index means sum over that index) is denoted simpy by its components $A^1,\ldots, A^k$. We have that $A(f)=A^j \partial_jf$.

Some more confusing notation
  • The index for coordinates of vector-fields is a superscript.
  • The index for the vector-fields of the local frame is a subscript.
  • Sometimes, given local coordinates, $X^j_{,h}$ or $X^j_{;h}$ or $\partial_hX^j$ denotes the partial derivative of the function $X^j$ with respect to the local coordinate $x^h$. This is NOT invariant with respect to a change of coordinates.
Commutator of two vector-fields

Given $X,Y\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$, their commutator (or Lie bracket) is denoted by $[X,Y]$ and is a vector-field defined by $$[X,Y](f)=X(Y(f))-Y(X(f))\;.$$ If $X=X^j\partial_j$ and $Y=Y^j\partial_j$, then $$[X,Y]^j\partial_jf=X^h\partial_h(Y^j\partial_jf)-Y^h\partial_h(X^j\partial_jf)=X^h(\partial_h Y^j)\partial_jf-Y^h(\partial_hX^j)\partial_jf$$ so $[X,Y]^j=X^h\partial_hY^j - Y^h\partial_h X^j$.

The Lie bracket has a number of reasonable properties, among which the Jacobi idenitity $$[X,[Y,Z]]+[Z,[X,Y]]+[Y,[Z,X]]=0\quad \forall\,X,Y,Z\in\mathfrak{X}(M)\;.$$ The Lie bracket turns the real vector space $\mathfrak{X}(M)$ into a (real) Lie algebra (whatever that means).

Flow of a vector-field

Let $X\in \mathcal{X}(M)$ be a vector-field; given an interval $I\subset\mathbb{R}$, a curve $\gamma:I\to M$ is an integral curve for $X$ if $$X_{\gamma(t)}=\dot{\gamma}(t)\quad \forall\,t\in I\;.$$

Given $V\subseteq M$ and $I\subseteq\mathbb{R}$, a map $\Phi:I\times V \to M$ is called local flow of $X$ if
  • $\Phi(0,p)=p$ for $p\in V$
  • $\phi_p:I\to M$ given by $\phi_p(t)=\Phi(t,p)$ is an integral curve for $X$ for all $p\in V$.
Flows and ODEs

Let $X$ be a vector-field and suppose we fixed a local frame (as we will always be doing). If $\gamma$ is an integral curve for $X$, then $X_{\gamma(t)}=\cdot{\gamma}(t)$, which means that $X_{\gamma(t)}(f)=(f\circ \gamma)'(t)$; if we take $f$ as a coordinate function $x^j$, then $$X^j_{\gamma(t)}=X_{\gamma(t)}(x^j)=(x^j\circ\gamma)'(t)\;.$$ If we express $\gamma$ in local coordinates, then $x^j\circ\gamma$ is just the $j$-th component of $\gamma$, so we end up with $k$ ODEs of the form $$X^j(\gamma_1,\ldots, \gamma_k)=(\gamma_j)'$$ where $X^j$ are smooth functions from an open set of $\mathbb{R}^k$ to $\mathbb{R}$.

One-parameter groups

If we fix the variable $t$ instead, we obtain $\phi^{(t)}:V\to M$ given by $\phi^{(t)}(p)=\Phi(t,p)$; notice that, if $t,s,t+s\in I$ and if $\phi^{(s)}(p)\in V$, then $$\phi^{(t)}\circ\phi^{(s)}(p)=\phi^{(t+s)}(p)\;.$$

A one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms is a collection of diffeomorphisms $\phi^{(t)}:M\to M$ for $t\in\mathbb{R}$ such that
  1. $\phi^{(t)}\circ\phi^{(s)}=\phi^{(t+s)}$ for all $t,s\in\mathbb{R}$
  2. $\phi^{(0)}(p)=p$ for all $p\in M$
  3. $(t,p)\mapsto \phi^{(t)}(p)$ is smooth from $\mathbb{R}\times M$ to $M$.

Given a one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms $\{\phi^{(t)}\}_t$, consider the curve $\gamma_p(t)=\phi^{(t)}(p)$; the vector-field $X_p=\dot{\gamma}_p(0)$ is called infinitesimal generator of the one-parameter groupd.

A global flow of a vector-field $X$ is a local flow defined on $\mathbb{R}\times M$.

Every one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms is given by the global flow of its infinitesimal generator.

Lie derivative

Let $X$ be a vector-field on $M$ and $f$ a smooth function; denote by $\Phi$ a local flow of $X$ near $p\in M$ and by $\phi^{(t)}$ the diffeomorphisms obtained by fixing $t$.

The Lie derivative of $f$ along $X$ is $$(\mathcal{L}_Xf)(p)=\frac{d}{d t} {\bigg\vert}_{t=0} ((\phi^{(t)})^*f)(p)=\lim_{t\to 0}\frac{((\phi^{(t)})^*f)(p)-f(p)}{t}\;.$$

Unravelling the definitions, $\mathcal{L}_Xf=Xf$.

Lie derivative of a vector-field

If $Y$ is another vector-field on $M$, we define its Lie derivative in the same way: $$\left(\mathcal{L}_XY\right)_p=\frac{d}{d t} {\bigg\vert}_{t=0} ((\phi^{(t)})^*Y)_p$$ however, it is more convenient to use a pushforward, as we are dealing with a vector-field, so we notice that for a diffeomorphism $\psi^*=(\psi^{-1})_*$ and $(\phi^{(t)})^{-1}=\phi^{(-t)}$, so $$\left(\mathcal{L}_XY\right)_p=\frac{d}{d t} {\bigg\vert}_{t=0} ((\phi^{(-t)})_*Y)_p$$ which gives $\mathcal{L}_XY=[X,Y]$.

Some properties
  • The Lie derivative is a derivation on $\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$
  • The Lie derivative is a derivation on $\mathfrak{X}(M)$ (as a $\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$-module), i.e. $\mathcal{L}_X(fY)=(\mathcal{L}_Xf)Y+f\mathcal{L}_XY$
  • It is compatible with the Lie bracket: $\mathcal{L}_X[Y,Z]=[\mathcal{L}_XY,Z]+[Y,\mathcal{L}_XZ]$
  • It is compatible with the differential: $\mathcal{L}_Xdf=d(\mathcal{L}_Xf)$

Differential $1$-forms

The set of differential $1$-forms on $M$ is $\Gamma(M, T^*M)$ and it is sometimes denoted by $\Omega^1(M)$. In $\mathbb{R}^k$, we write a differential $1$-form as $$\alpha=\alpha_1(p)dx^1+\ldots+\alpha_k(p)dx^k=\alpha_jdx^j$$ where $dx^1,\ldots, dx^k$ is the dual basis of $\partial_1,\ldots, \partial_k$. Therefore, given a (local or global) frame, we obtain a corresponding basis for $T_p^*M$, called a (local or global) coframe.

Given $X\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$ and $\alpha\in\Omega^1(M)$, once we fix a local frame and take components with respect to it, we have that $$\alpha(X)=\alpha_jX^j\;.$$

Pullback

Given $\alpha\in\Omega^1(M)$ and $\phi:N\to M$ a smooth map, $\phi^*\alpha$ is a $1$-form on $N$; if $p\in N$, $q\in M$ are such that $\phi(p)=q$ and we take local coordinates $x^1,\ldots, x^h$ around $p$ and $y^1,\ldots, y^k$ around $q$, then we can write $phi$ as $y^j=\phi^j(x^1,\ldots,x^h)$ for $j=1,\ldots, h$. Therefore, $$(\phi^*\alpha)_j=\alpha_i\circ\phi(\partial_j\phi^i)$$ where $(\partial_j\phi^i)$ is the Jacobian matrix of $\phi$ (note: we are multiplying it by the "row vector" $\alpha_i$).

In contrast, we recall that, if $X_p\in T_pN$, then $\phi_*X_p$ is given by $(\partial_j\phi^i)X^j_p$, i.e. we are multiplying the Jacobian matrix by the "column vector" $X^j$.

Lie derivative of a differential $1$-form

Given a vector-field $X\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$ and a differential $1$-form $\alpha\in\Omega^1(M)$, the Lie derivative of $\alpha$ along $X$ is given by $$\mathcal{L}_X\alpha=\frac{d}{dt}(\phi^{(t)})^*\alpha\bigg\vert_{t=0}$$ where $\phi^{(t)}(p)=\Phi(t,p)$ is the one parameter group of diffeomorphisms induced by the flow of $X$.

We have that $$(\mathcal{L}_X\alpha)_j=\alpha_i\partial_jX^i+X^j\partial_j\alpha^i\quad\Rightarrow\quad\mathcal{L}_X\alpha=\alpha_jdX^j+X(\alpha_i)dx^i$$ and $$(\mathcal{L}_X\alpha)(Y)=X(\alpha(Y))-\alpha([X,Y])=\mathcal{L}_X(\alpha(Y))-\alpha([X,Y])$$
