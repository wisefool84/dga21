Lecture 05

Abstract manifolds

We already saw as $k$-dimensional submanifolds of $\mathbb{R}^n$ can be characterized as subsets that are locally diffeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^k$.

This makes sense in $\mathbb{R}^n$ because we already have a notion of smooth map. Now, let $M$ be a generic set.

A coordinate chart for $M$ is the pair $(U,\phi)$ where $U\subset M$ and $\phi$ is a bijection between $U$ and an open set $V$ of $\mathbb{R}^k$.

Two charts $(U_1,\phi_1)$ and $(U_2,\phi_2)$ for $M$ are compatible if

$U_1\cap U_2=\emptyset$


or

$U_1\cap U_2\neq\emptyset$ and

  1. $\phi_1(U_1\cap U_2)$ and $\phi_2(U_1\cap U_2)$ are open sets in $\mathbb{R}^k$
  2. $\phi_2\circ\phi_1^{-1}:\phi_1(U_1\cap U_2)\to\phi_2(U_1\cap U_2)$ is a diffeomorphism.
Smooth structure

An atlas for $M$ is a collection of compatible charts $\{(U_j, \phi_j)\}_{j\in J}$ such that the sets $U_j$ cover all of $M$ for $j\in J$. Two atlases are compatible if their union is again an atlas

A smooth structure on $M$ is a maximal atlas. A smooth $k$-dimensional manifold is a set $M$ endowed with a smooth structure whose charts take values in $\mathbb{R}^k$.

Given two manifolds $M$, $N$, a map $F:M\to N$ is smooth if, for any $p\in M$ and any two charts $(U,\phi)$ and $(W,\psi)$ on $M$ and $N$ respectively, such that $p\in U$ and $F(p)\in W$, the map $$\psi\circ F\circ \phi^{-1}:\phi(U)\to \psi(W)$$ is smooth as a map between open sets in the Euclidean space.
Technicalities
  • On $\mathbb{R}^n$ there are infinitely many smooth structures (how many?).
  • All these structures are diffeomorphic, if $n\neq 4$.
  • On $\mathbb{R}^4$ there are smooth structures which are not diffeomorphic...
  • A smooth structure on a set $M$ gives also a topology on it, which is the unique topology such that, if $(U,\phi)$ is a chart, then $U$ is open in $M$ and $\phi$ is continuous.

Tangent vectors

We draw inspiration from the parallel between tangent vectors and directional derivatives for submanifolds of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Let $M$ be a smooth manifold and $p\in M$ a point; we denote by $\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$ the ring of smooth maps from $M$ to $\mathbb{R}$.

A tangent vector to $M$ at $p$ is a linear functional $X_p:\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)\to\mathbb{R}$ such that $$X_p(fg)=f(p)X_p(g)+g(p)X_p(f)\;.$$ Such a functional is usually called a derivation on $\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$ at $p$.

We denote by $T_pM$ the vector space of all the tangent vectors to $M$ at $p$.

What happens in a chart?

Let $(U,\phi)$ be a chart around $p$, with $\phi(p)=0\in\mathbb{R}^k$. Given $u\in\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$, we define $$(\phi_*X_p)(u)=X_p(\phi^*u)=X_p(u\circ\phi)$$ and we obtain a derivation on $\mathcal{C}^\infty(\mathbb{R}^k)$ at $0$. Therefore $$(\phi_*X_p)(u)=\frac{\partial u}{\partial v}(0)=(v^1\partial_1 u+\ldots+v^k\partial_k u)(0)$$ for some $v\in\mathbb{R}^k$.

On $\R^k$, the global vector-fields given by the $k$ partial derivatives are a basis of $T_x\R^k$ for each $x$; they are called a global frame.

It is easy to check that $\phi_*$ gives an isomorphism between $T_pM$ and $\mathbb{R}^k$.

The tangent bundle

The set $$TM=\{(p,X_p)\ :\ p\in M,\ X_p\in T_pM\}$$ can be given a natural smooth structure with charts of the form $(U\times\mathbb{R}^k, (\phi,\phi_*))$, where $(U,\phi)$ is a chart for $M$. The projection $\pi:TM\to M$ is a smooth map (a submersion).

A tangent vector-field on $M$ is a smooth section for $\pi$, i.e. a smooth map $X:M\to TM$ such that $X(p)=X_p\in T_pM$ for all $p\in M$.

Given a smooth map $F:M\to N$ between manifolds, we define $d_pF:T_pM\to T_{F(p)}N$ as $$dF_p(X_p)=(F_*X_p)(f)=(X_p\circ F^*)(f)=X_p(f\circ F)$$ for all $f\in\mathcal{C}^\infty(N)$. The map $dF:TM\to TN$ (also denoted by $F_*$) is smooth and it is called differential of $F$ (or tangent map or pushforward).

Vector-fields in coordinates

Let $M$ be a $k$-dimensional manifold. We denote the set of vector-fields on $M$ by $\mathfrak{X}(M)$ or by $\Gamma(M,TM)$ (i.e. sections of $TM$ defined on $M$).

If $(U,\phi)$ is a chart for $M$, we denote by $x^1,\ldots, x^k$ the components of $\phi$ (it takes values in $\R^k$). Then we consider on $M$ the vector-fields $\partial_1,\ldots, \partial_k$ such that $\partial_j(x^i)=\delta_j^i$. For $f\in\mathcal{C}^\infty(\R^k)$, $\phi_*(\partial_j)f=\partial_jf$ (where the RHS is the usual partial derivative).

Every $X\in\Gamma(U,TM)$ can be written as $X^1(p)\partial_1+\ldots+X^k(p)\partial_k$ or, compactly, as $X^j\partial_j$; $\{\partial_1,\ldots,\partial_k\}$ give a basis of $T_pM$ for $p\in U$. They are called a local frame.

Local computations

Once we have a local frame, we can perform local computations. If we are dealing with the "right" quantities, results will be independent of the local frame we chose, so we optimistically omit it.

Therefore, the vector-field $A=A^j\partial_j$ (Einstein summation: repeated index means sum over that index) is denoted simpy by its components $A^1,\ldots, A^k$. We have that $A(f)=A^j \partial_jf$.

Some more confusing notation
  • The index for coordinates of vector-fields is a superscript.
  • The index for the vector-fields of the local frame is a subscript.
  • Sometimes, given local coordinates, $X^j_{,h}$ or $X^j_{;h}$ or $\partial_hX^j$ denotes the partial derivative of the function $X^j$ with respect to the local coordinate $x^h$. This is NOT invariant with respect to a change of coordinates.
Push-forward in coordinates

Given $F:M\to N$ and a vector-field $X$ on $M$, we know that $Y=F_*X$ is a vector-field on $N$, such that, for every $f\in\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$, $Yf=F_*Xf=X(f\circ F)$. Now, given local coordinates $x^1,\ldots, x^k$ on $M$ and $y^1,\ldots, y^h$ on $N$, we have local frames $\partial_{x^i}$, $i=1,\ldots, k$ and $\partial_{y^j}$, $j=1,\ldots, h$ for $TM$ and $TN$ resp.

By definition , $Y=Y(y^j)\partial_{y^j}=X(y^j\circ F)\partial_{y^j}=X^i\partial_{x^i}y^j\circ F\partial_{y^j}$, so, writing $y^j=F^j(x^1,\ldots, x^k)$ (the expression of $F$ in local coordinates), we have $$Y^j=(\partial_iF^j)X^i$$ where $(\partial_iF^j)$ gives the Jacobian matrix of the expression of $F$ in local coordinates.

The differential of a scalar function

Let $f\in\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$, then $df_p$ is a linear functional from $T_pM$ to $\mathbb{R}$, i.e. an element of the dual space $T_p^*M$, which is called cotangent space; its elements are called cotangent vectors. The collection of cotangent spaces is $$T^*M=\{(p, \alpha_p)\ :\ p\in M,\ \alpha_p\in T^*_pM\}$$ it is called cotangent bundle, with the natural projection $\pi:T^*M\to M$.

A differential $1$-form on $M$ is a section of the cotangent bundle, i.e. a smooth map $\alpha:M\to T^*M$ such that $\alpha(p)=\alpha_p\in T_p^*M$ for all $p\in M$.
Pullback

Given a smooth map $F:M\to N$ between manifolds, we define the pullback $$F^*:T^*_{F(p)}N\to T^*_pM$$ as the dual of the pushforward $F_*$, so, given $\alpha_{F(p)}\in T^*_{F(p)}N$, $$F^*(\alpha_{F(p)})(X_p)=\alpha_{F(p)}(F_*X_p)$$ for every $X_p\in T_pM$.

If $\alpha$ is a differential $1$-form on $N$, then $F^*\alpha$ is the differential $1$-form on $M$ defined by $$(F^*\alpha)_p=F^*(\alpha_{F(p)})\;.$$

Integration on curves

Let $I=[0,1]$ and $\gamma:I\to M$ be a smooth map. Given $\alpha$ a differential $1$-form on $M$, we define $$\int_\gamma \alpha=\int_{0}^1\gamma^*\alpha=\int_0^1\alpha(\dot{\gamma}(t))dt\;.$$

We note that, if $u$ is another coordinate on $[0,1]$ (i.e. $u(t)$ is a reparametrization of $\gamma$), then the integral above is equal to $$\int_0^1\alpha(\dot{\gamma}(u))du\;.$$

In particular, $$\int_\gamma df=f(\gamma(1))-f(\gamma(0))\;.$$
