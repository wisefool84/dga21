Lecture 10

Minimal geodesics

Let us consider a Riemannian manifold $(M,g)$. We recall that we have a distance on $M$ given by $$\mathrm{dist}_g(p,q)=\inf\{L_g(\gamma)\ :\ \gamma:[a,b]\to M,\ \gamma(a)=p,\ \gamma(b)=q\}\;.$$

A curve $\gamma:[a,b]\to M$ is a minimizing geodesic if $$L_g(\gamma)=\mathrm{dist}_g(\gamma(a),\gamma(b))\;.$$

It is not obivious at all that minimizing geodesics exsist.

Euler-Lagrange equation

We want to minimize the length of the curve $\gamma$ such that $\gamma(a)=p$, $\gamma(b)=q$, i.e. the functional $$L_g(\gamma)=\int_{a}^b\sqrt{g_\gamma(\dot{\gamma},\dot{\gamma})}dt=\int_{a}^bS(\gamma,\dot{\gamma})dt\;.$$

We work in local coordinates, which, as we are only interested in what happens along $\gamma$, will all be functions of the parameter $t$. We will write $x^j(t)$ instead of $x^j(\gamma(t))$, to simplify the notation.

So $S(t,x,\dot{x})=\sqrt{g_{ij}\dot{x}^i\dot{x}^j}$. We have $$\frac{\partial S}{\partial x^h}=\frac{1}{2S}\partial_hg_{ij}\dot{x}^i\dot{x}^j\qquad \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial S}{\partial\dot{x}^h}=\frac{d}{dt}\frac{1}{S}g_{ih}\dot{x}^i$$ and if $\gamma$ is parametrized wrt arc length, then $$\frac{\partial S}{\partial x^h}=\frac{1}{2}\partial_hg_{ij}\dot{x}^i\dot{x}^j\qquad\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial S}{\partial\dot{x}^h}=\partial_pg_{ih}\dot{x}^p\dot{x}^i+g_{ih}\ddot{x}^i\;.$$

Geodesics equation

The Euler-Lagrange equations for the critical points of $L_g(\gamma)$ are $$\ddot{x}^p=\frac{1}{2}g^{ph}\partial_hg_{ij}\dot{x}^i\dot{x}^j-g^{ph}\partial_jg_{ih}\dot{x}^i\dot{x}^j=-g^{ph}\frac{1}{2}\left(\partial_jg_{ih}+\partial_ig_{ih}-\partial_hg_{ij}\right)\dot{x}^i\dot{x}^j=-\Gamma^p_{ij}\dot{x}^i\dot{x}^j\;.$$

We recognized (!) the Christoffel symbol of the Levi-Civita connection.

A curve $\gamma:I\to M$ is a geodesic if it satisfies the equations $$\ddot{\gamma}^h=-\Gamma^h_{ij}\dot{\gamma}^i\dot{\gamma}^j\qquad h=1,\ldots, k\;,$$ where $\gamma^i=x^i\circ\gamma$ in local coordinates.
Existence of geodesics

For every $p\in M$ there exist a neighborhood $U$ and $\epsilon>0$ such that for all $q\in U$ and $v\in T_qM$ with $|v|_g<\epsilon$ there is a geodesic $\gamma:(-1,1)\to M$ such that $\gamma(0)=q$ and $\dot{\gamma}(0)=v$.

For every $p\in M$ there exist a neighborhood $U$ and $\epsilon>0$ such that for any $q,q'\in U$ there is a geodesic $\gamma$ from $q$ to $q'$ of length less than $\epsilon$. Moreover $L_g(\gamma)=\mathrm{dist}_g(q,q')$.

If $\gamma:[a,b]\to M$ is parametrized wrt arc length and $L_g(\gamma)=\mathrm{dist}_g(\gamma(a),\gamma(b))$, then $\gamma$ is a geodesic (hence a minimizing geodesic).

Covariant derivative along a curve

Given a connection $v:TTM\to VTM$, we can define a covariant derivative of a vector-field along a curve, which works exactly as the usual covariant derivative.

Let $\gamma:I\to M$ be a curve; a vector-field along $\gamma$ is a map $X:I\to TM$ such that $\pi(X(t))=\gamma(t)$ for all $t\in I$. The covariant derivative of $X$ along $\gamma$ is the vector-field along $\gamma$ given by $$\frac{D}{dt}X=\mathrm{pr}_2\circ v\circ dX (d/dt)\;.$$

If $X$ can be extended to a neighborhood of $\gamma$ as a vector-field, then $$\frac{D}{dt}X(t)=\left(\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}(t)}X\right)_{\gamma(t)}\;.$$

Some properties

This covariant derivative shares essentially the algebraic properties of the other one:

  1. it is linear
  2. Leibniz rule holds: $\displaystyle{\frac{D}{dt}(fX)(t)=f(t)\frac{D}{dt}X(t)+f'(t)X(t)}$
  3. if $v$ is the Levi-Civita connection $$\frac{d}{dt}g(X(t),Y(t))=g\left(\frac{D}{dt}X(t),Y(t)\right)+g\left(X(t),\frac{D}{dt}Y(t)\right)\;.$$

A vector-field along $\gamma$ is a horizontal lift of $\gamma$ if and only if $\displaystyle\frac{D}{dt}X\equiv 0$; such a vector-field is said to be parallel along $\gamma$. A parallel vector-field is unique once we specify its value at one point. If $X$ is parallel, then $X_{\gamma(t)}$ is the parallel transport of $X_{\gamma(t_0)}$ along $\gamma$.

Geodesics - take 2

A curve is parametrized wrt arc length if and only if $g(\dot{\gamma},\dot{\gamma})\equiv 1$, so in particular the derivative in $t$ vanishes identically, so $$g\left(\frac{D}{dt}\dot{\gamma}(t),\dot{\gamma}(t)\right)=0\;.$$

In local coordinates, $$\left(\frac{D}{dt}\dot{\gamma}\right)^j=\nabla^j_{\dot{\gamma}}\dot{\gamma}=\partial_i\dot{\gamma}^j\dot{\gamma}^i+\dot{\gamma}^i\Gamma^j_{ih}\dot{\gamma}^h=\ddot{\gamma}^j+\Gamma^j_{ih}\dot{\gamma}^i\dot{\gamma}^h\;.$$

Therefore, $\gamma$ is a geodesic if and only if $$\frac{D}{dt}\dot{\gamma}=0\qquad\textrm{or equivalently}\qquad\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}\dot{\gamma}=0\;.$$

Exponential map

For $p\in M$ and $v\in T_pM$, $\gamma_v$ is the maximal solution to the geodesic equation such that $\gamma_v(0)=p$, $\dot{\gamma}_v(0)=v$; we denote by $\Omega$ the set of $(p,v)\in TM$ for which $\gamma_v(1)$ exists. $\Omega\subseteq TM$ is open and contains $0_p\in T_pM$ for all $p\in M$.

The exponential map $\exp:\Omega\to M$ is defined by $\exp(p,v)=\gamma_v(1)$, with $\gamma_v(0)=p$. We denote by $\exp_p$ the restriction of $\exp$ to $\Omega\cap T_pM$.

If $M$ is an open set in $\mathrm{GL}_n(\R)$, then $\exp:TM\to M$ is the matrix exponential. This holds also when $M$ is an open set of a linear subspace (e.g. the symmetric matrices) or is closed under multiplication and inverse (e.g. orthogonal matrices).

Properties and consequences

The map $\exp$ is smooth and $\exp_p$ is a local diffeomorphism from a neighborhood of $0_p\in T_pM$ to a neighborhood of $p\in M$. In particular, $(d\exp_p)_{0_p}=I$.

Therefore, $\exp$ is a first-order retraction.

Given $V_p$ neighborhood of $0_p$ in $T_pM$ and $U_p=\exp_p(V_p)$ neighborhood of $p$ in $M$, such that $\exp_p:V_p\to U_p$ is a diffeomorphism, then $(U_p, \exp_p^{-1})$ is a chart for $M$. In its local coordinates, called normal coordinates,

  1. geodesics from $p$ are just $\gamma_v(t)=tv$
  2. $g_{ij}(0)=\delta_{ij}$
  3. $\Gamma^k_{ij}(0)=0$
  4. $\partial_kg_{ij}(0)=0$.

Completeness

The following are equivalent

  • $\exp_p$ can be defined on all of $T_p$M for some $p\in M$
  • there is a minimzing geodesic between any two points of $M$
  • every geodesic can be extended to $\R$
  • $\exp$ is defined on all of $TM$
  • $(M,\mathrm{dist}_g)$ is (metrically) complete.

Taylor expansion along geodesics

Let $\gamma:(-1,1)\to M$ be a geodesic and let $f\in\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$. We consider the function $h:(-1,1)\to\R$ given by $h(t)=f(\gamma(t))$. We already know that $$h(t)=h(0)+tg_{\gamma(0)}(\mathrm{grad} f_{\gamma(0)}, \dot{\gamma}(0))+o(t)\;.$$ To proceed further, we need to compute $$h''(t)=\frac{d}{dt}h'(t)=\frac{d}{dt}g_{\gamma(t)}(\mathrm{grad} f_{\gamma(t)}, \dot{\gamma}(t))=g\left(\frac{D}{dt}\mathrm{grad}f,\dot{\gamma}\right)+g\left(\mathrm{grad} f,\frac{D}{dt}\dot{\gamma}\right)$$ but if $\gamma$ is a geodesic, the last term vanishes, so $$h''(t)=g\left(\frac{D}{dt}\mathrm{grad} f,\dot{\gamma}\right)=g(\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}} \mathrm{grad} f, \dot{\gamma})\;.$$

Hessian of a function

The bilinear form on $T_pM$ given by $(X_p,Y_p)\mapsto g(\nabla_{X_p} \mathrm{grad}f, Y_p)$ is known as Hessian of $f$ and denoted by $\nabla^2f$.

Therefore, on a geodesic $\gamma$, with $\gamma(0)=p$ and $\dot{\gamma}(0)=v$, $$f\circ \gamma(t)=f(p)+tg_p(\mathrm{grad} f_p,v)+\frac{t^2}{2}\nabla^2 f_p(v,v)+o(t^2)=f(p)+tdf_p(v)+\frac{t^2}{2}\nabla^2 f_p(v,v)+o(t^2)\;,$$ which is why the covariant derivative of a function is usually defined to be its differential: $\nabla f=df$. So that $$f(\gamma(t))=f(p)+t\nabla f_p(v)+\frac{t^2}{2}\nabla^2 f_p(v,v)+o(t^2)\;.$$

In local coordinates

We compute, $$\nabla^2 f(X,Y)=g(\nabla_X\mathrm{grad} f, Y)=X(g(\mathrm{grad}f, Y))-g(\mathrm{grad}f, \nabla_XY)=$$ $$\phantom{AA..}=X^j\partial_j(g_{pq}g^{pi}\partial_ifY^q)-g_{pq}g^{pi}\partial_i f(\partial_hY^q+\Gamma^q_{hj}Y^j)X^h$$ and we note that $g^{pq}g_{pi}=g^q_i=\delta^q_i$ so we obtain $$X^j\partial_j(\partial_ifY^i)-\partial_i f(\partial_hY^i+\Gamma^i_{hj}Y^j)X^h=\partial_i\partial_j f Y^iX^j-\partial_if\Gamma^i_{hj}X^hY^j\;.$$ Therefore, $\nabla^2 f= h_{ij}dx^i\otimes dx^j$ with $$h_{ij}=\partial_i\partial_jf- \Gamma^h_{ij}\partial_h f\;.$$
