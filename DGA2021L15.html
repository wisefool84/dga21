The divergence of a vector-field

Let $(M,g)$ be a Riemannian manifold.

Given $X\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$, its divergence is the function $\mathrm{div}X:M\to\R$ given by $$\mathrm{div}X=-\mathrm{tr}\nabla X\;.$$

Recall that, for each $p\in M$, $(\nabla X)_p$ is an endomorphism of $T_pM$, therefore its trace at $p$ is a well-defined value.

In local coordinates, $\nabla X=D^i_{j}dx^j\otimes\partial_i$ so $\mathrm{tr}(\nabla X)_p=D^{i}_i$ and more explicitly $$\mathrm{div} X(p)=-\partial_iX^i - \Gamma^i_{ji}X^j\;.$$

Covariant derivative of a differential form

Recall that a $1$-differential form is a section of $T^*M$, which at every $p$ gives a linear functional on $T_pM$. Using the metric, for $\alpha\in\Omega^1(M)$, we define $(\nabla \alpha)_p$ as the bilinear form on $T_pM$ given by $$(X_p,Y_p)\mapsto \nabla_{X_p}\alpha(Y_p)=g(\nabla_{X_p}\alpha^\sharp_p, Y_p)$$ or, more compactly, $\nabla_X\alpha=(\nabla_X\alpha^\sharp)^\flat$.

Accordingly, the codifferential of $\alpha$ is the function $\delta\alpha=-\mathrm{tr}\nabla\alpha^\sharp$.

Divergence for the Levi-Civita connection

If we suppose that our connection is the Levi-Civita connection, then the term $\Gamma^{i}_{ji}X^j$ from the divergence becomes $$\Gamma^i_{ji}X^j=g^{is}\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial g_{js}}{\partial x^i}+\frac{\partial g_{is}}{\partial x^j}-\frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial x_s}\right)X^j=\frac{1}{2}g^{is}\frac{\partial g_{is}}{\partial x^j}X^j\;.$$

Moreover, if $g=\det(g_{ij})$, then $$\partial_j g=\frac{\partial g}{\partial g_{is}}\frac{\partial g_{is}}{\partial x^j}$$

and $\partial g/\partial g_{is}$ is the minor of $g_{is}$ in the determinant expansion, so $$g^{is}=\frac{1}{g}\frac{\partial g}{\partial g_{is}}$$

so $\partial_j g=gg^{is}\partial_j g_{is}$. Substituting $$\mathrm{div}X=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}\partial_j(\sqrt{g} X^j)\;.$$

The Laplace-Beltrami operator

Let $(M,g)$ be a Riemannian manifold and $f:M\to\R$ a smooth function.

The Laplacian of $f$ is the function $\Delta f:M\to\R$ given by $$\Delta f=-\mathrm{div}\,\mathrm{grad} f=-\delta df\;.$$

The expression of $\Delta$ in local coordinates is not completely trivial; however if the connection is the Levi-Civita connection, then $$\Delta f=\frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}\partial_j(\sqrt{g} g^{ij}\partial_i f)\;.$$

Integration on a Riemannian manifold

In $\R^n$, the volume of the parallelotope generated by $a_1,\ldots, a_n$ is $$\sqrt{\det(\langle a_i, a_j\rangle)}$$ where $\langle\cdot ,\cdot \rangle$ is the standard scalar product.

This suggests to use, in a chart, the measure of volume $$d\,\mathrm{vol}_g=\sqrt{\det{g_{ij}}} dx^1\cdots dx^n\;,$$

which, indeed, is invariant under changes of charts such that $\det(D\phi\circ\psi^{-1})>0$. If we can cover the whole manifold with such charts, then we have a global volume measure.

Manifolds with such a property are called orientable. Now, we can integrate.

Smooth boundary

If $U\subseteq M$ is an open domain with smooth boundary, we can give the boundary $bU$ the structure of a smooth manifold of dimension $n-1$. If $M$ is a Riemannian manifold, $bU$ inherits the metric and, if $M$ is orientable, also $bU$ is, in a way that is compatible with the $(n-1)$-volume of $bU$, $d\,\mathrm{vol}_b$.

The fact that $bU$ is orientable implies that we have a global never-vanishing section of the normal bundle $NbU$ in $M$; we consider the unit norm section $\nu$ (the "inward/outward direction" is chosen canonically given the orientations of $M$ and $bU$).

With these assumptions, we have that if $U\Subset M$ then $$\int_{U}\mathrm{div} Xd\,\mathrm{vol}_g=-\int_{bU}g(X,\nu)d\,\mathrm{vol}_b\;.$$

Some properties of the Laplacian

We define $\partial_\nu f$ as $g(\mathrm{grad} f, \nu)$.

  1. $\Delta f=\mathrm{tr}\nabla^2 f$
  2. $\displaystyle{\int_{U}(f_1\Delta f_2-f_2\Delta d_1)d\,\mathrm{vol}_g=-\int_{bU}\left(f_1\partial_\nu f_2-f_2\partial_\nu f_1\right)d\,\mathrm{vol}_b}$
  3. if $M$ is compact connected and $\Delta f\geq 0$, then $f$ is constant
  4. if $M$ is compact connected, then
    $\displaystyle{\int_M f\Delta fd\,\mathrm{vol}_g=\int_{M}g(\mathrm{grad} f, \mathrm{grad} f)d\,\mathrm{vol}_g\geq 0}$

Spectral Theory

Let $(M,g)$ be a compact Riemannian manifold (or a complete manifold, where we consider only compactly supported objects).

The $L^2$-inner product on $\mathcal{C}^\infty_0(M)$ is $$(f_1,f_2)=\int_Mf_1(p)f_2(p)d\,\mathrm{vol}_g(p)$$ and the associated norm is $\|f\|=\sqrt{(f,f)}$.

Similarly, if $\mathfrak{X}_0(M)$ is the space of compactly supported vector-fields, we define the inner product for $X,Y\in\mathfrak{X}_0(M)$ as $$(X,Y)=\int_Mg_p(X_p,Y_p)d\,\mathrm{vol}_g(p)\;.$$

Reformulation

Some of the properties we stated before can be reformulated as

  • $(f,\mathrm{div} X)=-(\mathrm{grad} f, X)$
  • $(\Delta f_1, f_2)=(\mathrm{grad} f_1,\mathrm{grad} f_2)=(f_1,\Delta f_2)$
  • $(\Delta f, f)\geq 0$

In the appropriate setting, $\Delta:\mathcal{C}^\infty_0(M)\to\mathcal{C}^\infty_0(M)$ can be extended as a self-adjoint positive definite linear operator on a (dense domain inside a) Hilbert space (inner product space, possibly infinite dimensional).

Dirichlet eigenvalue problem

In order to apply the Spectral theorem to $\Delta$, we need to find its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. These are the solutions of the differential equation $$\left\{\begin{array}{rcl}\Delta u=\lambda u&\textrm{ on }& U\\u=0&\textrm{ on }& bU\end{array}\right.$$

Under our hypotheses, this problem has a discrete set of solutions $\lambda$, each with finite multiplicity. For example, if $M$ is compact, $\lambda=0$ is the smallest eigenvalue and the eigenspace is the space of constant functions.

Eigenmaps

The functions belonging to each eigenspace are called eigenfunctions. We discard the smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_0=0$ and we denote by $\lambda_1\leq \lambda_2\leq\ldots$ the positive eigenvalues of $\Delta$, repeated according to multiplicity.

Suppose that $u_1,\ldots, $ are corresponding eigenfunctions, which give an orthonormal system.

Consider the map, for $m\in\mathbb{N}$, $$\Phi^m:M\to\R^m\qquad \Phi^m(x)=(u_1(x),\ldots, u_m(x))\;.$$

For $m$ large enough, $\Phi^m$ is a smooth embedding.
