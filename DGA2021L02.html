Lecture 02

Calculus on submanifolds

Let $M$ be a $k$-dimensional submanifold of $\mathbb{R}^n$ and let $f:M\to\mathbb{R}$ be a $\mathcal{C}^1$-smooth function. We want to define the gradient of $f$.

As a starting point, we generalize the directional derivatives. However, we do not have straight lines on $M$ along which computing such derivatives, so the fist attempt could be to define the directional derivative along a curve.

A curve (in $M$) through $p$ is, for us, a smooth map $\gamma:(-1,1)\to M$ such that $\gamma(0)=p$. There are a lot of curves! Before diving into such complexity, we may try something easier:

The tangent space to $M$ at $p$ is the set of all tangent vectors at $p$ to all the curves in $M$ through $p$.

$$T_pM=\{\dot{\gamma}(0)\ \vert\ \gamma:(-1,1)\to M \textrm{ smooth, }\gamma(0)=p\}\;.$$

The tangent space
  • $T_p\mathbb{R}^n=\mathbb{R}^n$ (and the same is true for an affine subspace)
  • By the local characterization of submanifolds, $T_pM=\mathbb{R}^k$ for all $p\in M$.
  • If $F:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^{n-k}$ is a defining function for $M$, then $T_pM=\ker DF_p$.

Given $v\in T_pM$, the directional derivative of $f$ along $v$ is $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial v}(p)=\frac{d}{dt}(f(\gamma(t)))\vert_{t=0}$$ for any $\gamma:(-1,1)\to M$ such that $\gamma(0)=p$, $\dot{\gamma}(0)=v$.

Gradient of a function

We consider the map $$v\mapsto \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}(p)$$

It can easily seen to be linear (via local characterization, for example). Therefore, by Riesz theorem, there exists an element $\nabla^Mf_p\in T_pM$ (an Euclidean subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$) such that $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial v}(p)=\langle \nabla^Mf_p, v\rangle\;.$$ We call such vector the gradient of $f$ (along $M$).

For each $p\in M$, we consider the orthogonal projection $\mathrm{Pr}_p:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n$ whose image is $T_pM$. It is easy to see that $$\nabla^Mf_p=\mathrm{Pr}_p\nabla f_p$$ where $\nabla$ is the usual gradient in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and we extended $f$ to a neighborhood of $M$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Extremal points

Exactly as in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the search for maximum and mimimum points of a function(al) on $M$ means to look for singular points, critical points and boundary points. In particular, critical points are $p\in M$ such that $\nabla^Mf_p=0$. If $F:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^{n-k}$ is a local defining function for $M$ near $p$, $$\nabla^Mf_p=0\Leftrightarrow \nabla f_p\perp T_pM\Leftrightarrow \nabla f_p\perp\ker DF_p\Leftrightarrow \nabla f_p\in \mathrm{Im} (DF_p^T)\;.$$ which is exactly the condition on the gradients in the Lagrange multipliers system.

The tangent bundle

The collection of all the tangent spaces to a manifold is itself a manifold.

Given $M\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ a $k$-dimensional submanifold, its tangent bundle is $$TM=\{(p,v)\in\mathbb{R}^n\times\mathbb{R}^n\ :\ p\in M,\ v\in T_pM\}$$ together with the map $\pi:TM\to M$, which is the natural projection.

If $F:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^{n-k}$ is a local defining function for $M$, then $G:\mathbb{R}^n\times\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^{n-k}\times\mathbb{R}^{n-k}$ given by $G(x,y)=(F(x), DF_x[y])$ is a local defining function for $TM$, which is a $2k$-dimensional manifold in $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$.

  • $T\mathbb{S}^1=\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{0\}$
  • $T\mathbb{S}^2=\{(z_0,z_1,z_2,z_3)\in\mathbb{C}^4\ :\ z_0^2+z_1^2+z_2^2+z_3^2=1\}$
The normal bundle

Another natural object to consider is the so called normal bundle, i.e. $$NM=\{(p,v)\in\mathbb{R}^n\times\mathbb{R}^n\ :\ p\in M,\ v\perp T_pM\}\;.$$ Again, we have a natural projection on $M$.

Obviously, $N_pM$ (i.e. the fiber of such projection over $p\in M$) is the orthogonal complement of $T_pM$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

  • $N\mathbb{S}^1=T\mathbb{S}^1$
  • $N\mathbb{S}^2=\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\}$
Submersions and sections

If $\pi:E\to M$ is a submersion between submanifolds and if $U\subset M$ is an open set, then a section of $\pi:E\to M$ over $U$ is a function $\sigma:U\to E$ such that $\pi(\sigma(p))=p$ for all $p\in U\,.$

A tangent vector-field on $M$ is a section of $\pi:TM\to M$; similarly, a normal vector field on $M$ is a section of $\pi:NM\to M$.

Usually, we are interested in sections with some regularity conditions.

The gradient vector-field

Given a smooth function $f:M\to\mathbb{R}$, we can extend it to a neighborhood $U$ of $M$.

  • $p\mapsto \nabla f_p$ is a smooth map from $U$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$
  • $p\mapsto\mathrm{Pr}_p$ is a smooth map from $M$ to $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$
  • $p\mapsto\mathrm{Pr}_p(\nabla f_p)=\nabla^Mf_p$ is a smooth map from $M$ to $TM$

i.e. $\nabla^Mf$ is a smooth section of $TM$, that is, a tangent vector-field.

The differential of a smooth map

Given two submanifolds $M\subseteq\mathbb{R}^m$ and $N\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n$ and a smooth map $F:M\to N$, for any $\gamma:(-1,1)\to M$, smooth curve through $p\in M$, the composition $\eta=F\circ\gamma$ gives a curve through $F(p)\in N$.

The differential of $F$ at $p$ is the linear map $$dF_p:T_pM\to T_{F(p)}N$$ such that $$dF_p[\dot{\gamma}(0)]=\dot{\eta}(0)\;.$$

$dF$ is a smooth map from $TM$ to $TN$.

If $dF_p$ is injective, the map $F$ is an immersion at $p$, if it is surjective, the map $F$ is a submersion at $p$. A map $F$ is a diffeomorphism if and only if $F$ is bijective and its differential is invertible at all points.

The differential of the gradient

Given $f:M\to\mathbb{R}$, the gradient $\nabla^Mf$ is a smooth map from $M$ to $TM$, whose differential has rank $k$ at all points, because $\pi\circ\nabla^Mf=\mathrm{Id}_M$ (where $\pi:TM\to M$ is the natural projection). Its differential is, in principle, a map from $TM\to T(TM)$; we note that $T_{(p,v)}TM=T_pM\times T_v(T_pM)$ and, because $T_pM$ is linear subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$, $T_v(T_pM)=T_pM$.

With respect to this splitting, $d\nabla^Mf$ acts as the identity from $T_pM$ to the first factor $T_pM$, so we only consider the "second component", i.e. the map $D\nabla^Mf:T_pM\to T_{\nabla^Mf_p}(T_pM)$.

The Hessian and the second fundamental form

The bilinear form $D^2f_p:T_pM\times T_pM\to\mathbb{R}$ given by $$D^2_Mf_p(v,w)=\langle v,D\nabla^Mf_p[w]\rangle$$ is symmetric and it is called the Hessian of $f$ on $M$.

If $D^2f$ is the usual Hessian of (an extestion of) $f$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$, then the symmetric bilinear form on $T_pM$ given by $$(v,w)\mapsto D^2_Mf_p(v,w)-D^2f_p(v,w)$$ can be seen as a linear function of $\nabla f_p$, i.e. there exists a bilinear map $H_p:T_pM\times T_pM\to N_pM$ such that $$\langle H_p(v,w), \nabla f_p\rangle=D^2_Mf_p(v,w)-D^2f_p(v,w)$$ such map $H:TM\times TM\to NM$ is called second fundamental form of $M$.
