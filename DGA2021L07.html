Lecture 07

Exterior product of $1$-forms

The theory of differential forms is developed to interact with the theory of integration; we want a product on $1$-forms that acts on $m$-tuples of vector-fields in a way compatible with the usual properties of the integral (e.g. change of variables).

Given $\alpha,\ \beta\in\Omega^1(M)$ and $X,Y\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$, we define $$\alpha\wedge\beta(X,Y)=\alpha(X)\beta(Y)-\beta(X)\alpha(Y)$$ and inductively we extend the definition to the product of $m$ differential $1$-forms $\alpha_1,\ldots, \alpha_m$: $$\alpha_1\wedge\ldots\wedge\alpha_m(X_1,\ldots, X_m)=\sum_{\sigma}\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)\alpha_1(X_{\sigma(1)})\cdots\alpha_m(X_{\sigma(m)})\;.$$ As a result, given an open domain $D\subseteq\mathbb{R}^m$ and $\phi:D\to M$ smooth, $$\int_{\phi(D)}\alpha_1\wedge\ldots\wedge\alpha_m=\int_D\alpha_1\wedge\ldots\wedge\alpha_m(\phi_*(\partial_1),\ldots,\phi_*(\partial_m))dx_1\wedge\ldots\wedge dx_m$$

Differential $m$-forms

We denote by $\Lambda^mT_p^*M$ the vector space of multilinear alternating maps from $(T_pM)^m$ to $\mathbb{R}$; $\Lambda^1T^*M=T^*M$ and $\Lambda^0T^*M=\mathbb{R}$. For instance, if $\dim T_p^M=k$, then $\Lambda^kT_p^*M$ is one dimensional and is generated by $\det$.

A differential $m$-form is a multilinear alternating functional on $m$-tuples of vector-fields, i.e. it is a section of $$\Lambda^mT^*M=\{(p,\alpha_p)\ :\ p\in M,\ \alpha_p\in\Lambda^mT^*_pM\}$$

The space of sections, i.e. the space of differential $m$-forms, is denoted by $\Omega^m(M)$; $\Omega^0(M)$ is the space of real-valued smooth functions on $M$, i.e. $\mathcal{C}^\infty(M)$.

Exterior product

Given $\alpha\in\Omega^p(M)$ and $\beta\in\Omega^m(M)$, we define the form $$\alpha\wedge\beta(X_1,\ldots, X_{p+m})=\sum_{\sigma}\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)\alpha(X_{\sigma(1)},\ldots, X_{\sigma(p)})\beta(X_{\sigma(p+1)},\ldots, X_{\sigma(p+m)})$$ where $\sigma$ ranges among all the permutations of $\{1,\ldots,p+m\}$ such that $$\sigma(1)\leq \ldots\leq \sigma(p) $$ $$\sigma(p+1)\leq\ldots\leq\sigma(p+m)\;.$$ The form $\alpha\wedge\beta$ is clearly multilinear and alternating, so it is an element of $\Omega^{p+m}(M)$.

Exterior differential

The exterior differential is the (unique) $\mathbb{R}$-linear map $d:\Omega^p(M)\to\Omega^{p+1}(M)$ such that

  • if $f\in\Omega^0(M)$, then $df$ is the differential
  • if $f\in\Omega^0(M)$, then $d(df)=0$
  • if $\alpha\in\Omega^p(M)$ and $\beta\in\Omega^m(M)$, then $d(\alpha\wedge\beta)=d\alpha\wedge\beta+(-1)^p\alpha\wedge d\beta$.

One can show that $$d\alpha(X_0,X_1,\ldots, X_p)=\sum_{i}(-1)^iX_i(\alpha(\ldots, \widehat{X}_i,\ldots)+\sum_{i < j}(-1)^{i+j}\alpha([X_i,X_j],\ldots, \widehat{X}_i,\ldots, \widehat{X}_j,\ldots)$$

Metrics

A scalar product on $T_pM$ is a bilinear symmetric positive definite form, i.e. a function from $T_pM\times T_pM$ to $\mathbb{R}$ which is bilinear, symmetric and positive definite; therefore, it is a particular element of $T_p^*M\otimes T_p^*M$ (the set of bilinear forms) which is symmetric and positive definite.

A Riemannian metric on $M$ is a smooth section $g:M\to T^*M\otimes T^*M$ such that, for any $p\in M$ and $X_p,\ Y_p\in T_pM$, we have $g_p(X_p,Y_p)=g_p(Y_p,X_p)$ and $g_p(X_p,X_p)>0$ for all $X_p\in T_pM\setminus\{0\}$.

Given a local frame, a metric is usually given as $g=g_{ij}dx^i\otimes dx^j$, where $g_{ij}=g_{ji}$. With this notations, $g(X,Y)=g_{ij}X^iY^j$.

Musical isomorphism

Given a vector-field $X\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$ and a Riemannian metric $g$ on $M$, we define the $1$-form $X^\flat\in\Omega^1(M)$ by $$X^\flat(Y)=g(X,Y)\qquad \forall\,Y\in\mathfrak{X}(M)\;.$$ In coordinates, $X^\flat=X^ig_{ij}dx^j$. This is called lowering the index or contraction of $g$ with $X$.

So, we have a map $\phantom{A}^\flat:TM\to T^*M$ which is an isomorphism of bundles (because $g$ is positive definite).

The inverse map is denoted by $\phantom{A}^\sharp:T^*M\to TM$ and will be given by a section of $TM\otimes TM$, again symmetric and positive definite, which we write, in a local frame, as $g^{ij}\partial_i\otimes\partial_j$. Therefore $$\alpha^\sharp=\alpha_ig^{ij}\partial_j$$ (this is obviously called raising the index) and $(X^\flat)^\sharp=X$, so $g^{ij}g_{ij}=\delta^i_j$.

The gradient of a function

A smooth $k$-dimensional manifold $M$ endowed with a Riemannian metric $g$ is called Riemannian manifold.

If $(M,g)$ is a Riemannian manifold and $f:M\to\mathbb{R}$ is a smooth function, then the gradient of $f$ is the vector-field $$\mathrm{grad}f=(df)^\sharp$$ i.e. with respect to a local frame $$\mathrm{grad}f=g^{ij}(\partial_i f) \partial_j\;.$$

Tensors

A covariant $m$-tensor on $T_pM$ is a real-valued multilinear function $$R_p:\underbrace{T_pM\times\ldots\times T_pM}{\textrm{$m$ times}}\to\mathbb{R}$$ and the set of all covariant $m$-tensors is denoted by $T^m(T_pM)$ and can be written as the tensor product $$\underbrace{T_p^*M\otimes\ldots\otimes T_p^*M}_{\textrm{$m$ times}}\;.$$ The bunlde $T^mM$ is defined by $$\{(p,R_p)\ :\ p\in M,\ R_p\in T^m(T_pM)\}$$ and its sections are called covariant $m$-tensor fields (usually "fields" is omitted).

Differential $m$-forms are a special kind of covariant $m$-tensors (alternating or skew-symmetric) as well as metrics, which are symmetric covariant $2$-tensors.

Contravariant tensors

A contravariant $h$-tensor on $T_pM$ is a real-valued multilinear function $$S_p:\underbrace{T_p^*M\times\ldots\times T_p^*M}_{\textrm{$h$ times}}\to\mathbb{R}$$ and the set of all contravariant $h$-tensors is denoted by $T_h(T_pM)$ and can be written as the tensor product $$\underbrace{T_pM\otimes\ldots\otimes T_pM}_{\textrm{$h$ times}}\;.$$ The bundle $T_h(M)$ is defined by $$\{(p,S_p)\ :\ p\in M,\ S_p\in T_h(T_pM)\}$$ and its sections are called contravariant $h$-tensor fields (usually "fields" is omitted).

The "inverse" metric $g^{ij}$ is a contravariant $2$-tensor.

Mixed tensors

The space of mixed tensors of type $(m,h)$ is $$T_h^m(T_pM)=\underbrace{T_p^*M\otimes \ldots\otimes T_p^*M}_{\textrm{$m$ times}}\otimes\underbrace{T_pM\otimes\ldots\otimes T_pM}_{\textrm{$h$ times}}$$ and the bunlde $T_h^m(M)$ and its sections are defined accordingly.

Example: given a vector space $V$, an endomorphism $L:V\to V$ is naturally an element of $T_1^1(V)$.

A mixed tensor of type $(m,h)$, in coordinates, will be written as $$A^{i_1,\ldots, i_h}_{j_1,\ldots, j_m}dx^{j_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes dx^{j_m}\otimes \partial_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes\partial_{i_h}$$

Nomima nuda tenemus

In modern terminology, a map $F:M\to N$ induces a covariant transformation (functor) from $TM$ to $TM$, i.e. the pushforward $F_*$, which acts on contravariant tensors; whereas it induces a contravariant transformation (functor) from $T^*N$ to $T^*M$, i.e. the pullback $F^*$, which acts on covariant tensors.

The names co- and contra-variant related to transformations denote their direction (along the original $F$ or in the opposite direction); the same names applied to vectors or tensors refers to the way their coordinates change when a map is applied to the vector space.
